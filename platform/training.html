Part 1 of 2

Jonathan — Estimating & Project Management

From bid invite to closeout. The modules that carry an estimate through to a finished, invoiced job.
Your Daily Routine
  1. Open the Dashboard — scan what's due, what's overdue, and which projects need a decision today.
  2. Check new bid invites in your inbox. For each one, run the Feasibility Tool before you spend any estimating time on it.
  3. Update the Bid Pipeline — move statuses forward (Reviewing → Estimating → Submitted), set follow-up dates.
  4. Work your active Estimating jobs — build or refine estimates due soonest first.
  5. Review Active Projects in the field — check the project checklist, flag anything stalled.
  6. Scan QA/QC data from yesterday's installs (GUHMA logs + modulus tests) for anything out of spec.
  7. Handle any Change Orders or Closeout items waiting on you.
Your Modules

1 Dashboard

Your morning landing page. Shows bids due, projects in progress, and anything needing action.

Do this: Open it first thing. If something is red or flagged, it needs you today.

2 Feasibility Tool BID REVIEW NO-BID

The first step on every new bid invite. Score the job before you spend estimating hours on it. Enter the key facts (soil, size, location, GC, timeline) and the tool returns a recommendation.

Why it matters: Estimating time is expensive. A 5-minute feasibility check tells you whether to chase it, look closer, or pass — so you spend your hours on bids you can actually win.

3 Bid Pipeline

Every bid we're tracking, with its current status. Your single source of truth for "what's in play."

Do this: Keep statuses current and set follow-up dates. A stale pipeline is a missed bid.

4 Estimating

Where you build the number. Uses the POET template with auto-calculation built in.

Working days = LF ÷ 1,000  •  Stone (tons) = LF × 0.209  •  Spoils = 1.5× LF (24") / 2× LF (30")

ConfirmLabor burden flag. Before you finalize, confirm workers' comp is included in the estimate. This is an open question — don't submit a number assuming it's baked in until you've verified it for that job.

5 Material Costs

Current stone pricing by supplier (Heidelberg, IMI, Martin Marietta, StoneCo/Shelly, Vulcan, and others).

Do this: Pull live pricing for the supplier nearest the job site before locking your estimate. Don't reuse an old number.

6 Active Projects

Manage work in progress through the 130-field project checklist — from mobilization through production.

Do this: Walk the checklist for each live job. It's your guardrail against a missed step.

7 QA/QC — GUHMA & Modulus Testing

Installation quality data. GUHMA logs every column; modulus testing verifies load capacity (jack cal factors: 49.84 small <175 kips, 32.88 large >175 kips).

Do this: Review daily. Out-of-spec results are easiest to fix while the crew is still on site.

8 Change Orders

When field conditions differ from the bid (deeper columns, harder soils, added scope), document and price the change here.

Do this: Raise the CO before the extra work is done. Unbilled scope is lost margin.

9 Closeout

The end-of-job workflow: GGG (Dr. Ed Garbin) documentation, final QA package, and final invoicing.

Do this: Don't let jobs sit "almost done." Close them out promptly to release final payment.

Workflow: New Bid → Award → Closeout
1
Bid invite arrives → Feasibility Tool

Score it first. BID, REVIEW, or NO-BID. Only proceed if it's worth the estimating time.

2
Log it → Bid Pipeline

Add the opportunity, set status to "Estimating," and note the due date.

3
Price it → Estimating + Material Costs

Build the POET estimate, pull current stone pricing, and confirm labor burden / workers' comp is included.

4
Submit → Bid Pipeline

Move status to "Submitted," set a follow-up date to chase the GC.

5
Award → Active Projects

Spin up the project and start working the 130-field checklist toward mobilization.

6
Install → QA/QC GUHMA + Modulus  (+ Change Orders as needed)

Log every column, verify modulus, and raise change orders the moment field conditions shift.

7
Finish → Closeout

Assemble the GGG document package, complete the QA record, and send the final invoice.

Derek — Business Development

Filling the pipeline. Getting in early with the right GCs and engineers, and turning conversations into bids.
Your Daily Routine
  1. Open the Dashboard / CEO Dashboard — check total pipeline value, win rate, and what's in play.
  2. Review the Bid Pipeline — who needs a follow-up call today? What's pending a GC decision?
  3. Pull up your target Subcontractors / contacts — pick 3-5 to reach out to (focus on data center GCs and geotech engineers).
  4. When a GC mentions a real opportunity, quick-score it live with the Feasibility Tool so you can speak to it credibly.
  5. Log every conversation and next step so nothing slips. Cultivate relationships before the bid, not after.
Your Modules

1 Dashboard / CEO Dashboard

Your bird's-eye view: total pipeline value, win rate, and what's currently in play. The CEO Dashboard rolls it up the way Brad sees it.

Do this: Start here daily. Know your numbers before you make a single call.

2 Bid Pipeline

What's bid, what's pending, and what needs a follow-up. Your action list for the day.

Do this: Work the follow-ups. A bid sitting un-chased is the easiest deal to lose.

3 Subcontractors & Contacts

Your GC and vendor contact base — who to call, who decides, and the relationship history.

AssetStart from the 468 BD contacts from Ray's network. These are warm relationships already in the system — don't cold-prospect from zero when you have a head start.

4 Feasibility Tool

Quick-score an opportunity the moment a GC describes it. Enter the basics and you'll know on the spot whether it's a real fit for VSC.

Do this: Use it live in conversations. Being able to say "that's a strong fit for us" in the moment builds credibility and momentum.

Strategic Guidance — Where to Aim

Data centers = the #1 market

$23B+ in data center investment in Indiana alone, including Google's project in our own Fort Wayne backyard. Prioritize data center GCs above everything else.

Get in early — before the bid

By the time a bid drops, the spec may already be written. Cultivate GCs and geotech engineers ahead of the bid so we're shaping it, not reacting to it.

Cultivate geotech engineers

Geopier's threat is at the spec stage — engineers write Geopier into specs. Build relationships with geotech engineers so VSC is on their radar before they put pen to paper.

Get VSC named as an accepted alternate

Where Geopier is already specced, the goal is to get VSC listed as an accepted alternate in the spec. That single line opens the door to bid.

North StarBe early, be everywhere data centers are, and own the engineer relationship. The bid is the last step of a relationship that should already exist.
Workflow: Working a New Lead
1
Spot the opportunity → Subcontractors / Contacts

Check Ray's 468-contact network first — is this GC or engineer already a warm relationship? Start from a name, not a stranger.

2
Qualify it → Feasibility Tool

Quick-score the job — soil, size, location, timeline. Know if it's a strong VSC fit before you invest the relationship.

3
Get in early → Geotech engineer + GC outreach

Reach the geotech engineer before the spec is finalized. Aim to get VSC named — or named as an accepted alternate to Geopier.

4
Track it → Bid Pipeline

Log the opportunity, set the relationship stage and next follow-up date. Hand a qualified lead to Jonathan to estimate.

5
Watch the numbers → CEO Dashboard

Track pipeline value and win rate over time. Double down on the lead types that actually convert.

HandoffWhen a lead is qualified and the relationship is warm, hand it to Jonathan — he picks it up at the Feasibility / Estimating step in Part 1. You fill the pipeline; he prices and delivers it.
